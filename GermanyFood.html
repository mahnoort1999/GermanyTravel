Delve into the world of German cuisine and you will discover an array of rich, hearty, and comfortable delicious dishes.

While each region of Germany has its own speciality dishes and traditional cuisine; certain culinary delights are cherished

by many people throughout the country. The dishes are delicious and easy to make, so it won't be difficult for you to

incorporate it into your weekly plan. So, why not explore German culture with your tastebuds and create your own German dishes.


Käsespätzle:

Germany's answer to pasta, Spätzle is popular in the south of the country.

This dish comprises of soft egg noodles and are made from wheat flour,

and egg. Topped off with Käsespätzle (cheese) and roasted onions. This dish

remains a Swabian speciality.


Eintopf:

Eintops is similar to Irish stew and this dish is found in many

different regions throughout Germany. It contains contain the

same basic ingredients: broth, vegetables, potatoes or pulses,

and pork, beef, chicken, or fish.


Kartoffelpuffer:

Kartoffelpuffer are shallow pan-fried pancakes made from grated

or ground potatoes mixed with flour, egg, onion, and seasoning.

You can top them with a variety of sweet or savory condiments,

such as sour cream, cottage cheese, apple sauce, or cinnamon.


Schnitzel:

Coated in breadcrumbs and often served with a slice of lemon,

this thin, boneless cutlet of meat is an iconic part of German cuisine.

You can choose a Wiener Schnitzel (Viennese style) made from veal,

or a Schnitzel Wiener Art, with pork (Schwein). If you order a

Hamburg-style schnitzel, it will arrive with a fried egg on top,

while a Holsten-style schnitzel comes with an egg, anchovies, and capers.


Brezel:

Great to eat as a side dish or snack, the Brezel (pretzel) is a type of

baked pastry that is made from a dough commonly shaped into a knot.

Brezel is usually sprinkled with salt, however, other seasonings

include cheese, sugar, chocolate, cinnamon, and different seeds.

This is a popular German food to eat with German beer and you will

find it in bakeries and on street stalls throughout the country.

They usually come plain, sliced, and buttered (Butterbrezel) or with

slices of cold meats or cheese.


Schwarzwälder Kirschtorte:

The delicious Schwarzwälder Kirschtorte (which literally means ‘Black Forest cherry torte’).

This dessert gets its name from Schwarzwälder Kirschwasser, a liqueur distilled from tart cherries.

interestingly, German law actually mandates that Kirschwasser must be present in the cake for it to be

labeled a Schwarzwälder Kirschtorte. Alternating layers of rich chocolate cake, cherries, and whipped cream

are topped off with more cream, maraschino cherries, and chocolate shavings for a luxurious finish.
